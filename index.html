1. The 7Cs of Communication (Completeness & Correctness Only)

Completeness

Definition: A business message is complete when it contains all the facts that the reader or listener needs for the exact reaction you desire. Communicators must remember that backgrounds and mindsets differ; what seems clear to the sender might be entirely inadequate for a receiver without adequate context.

Guidelines for Completeness:
1. Provide all necessary information: Ensure every factual detail required to safely execute a task is explicitly stated.
2. Answer all questions asked: Intentionally map and return an explicit answer to every user query.
3. Give something extra when desirable: Anticipate downstream roadblocks and provide proactive secondary solutions.
Bad Example (Incomplete)
Hi everyone,
I just wanted to send you all a reminder about the meeting we're having tomorrow! See you then,
Good Example (Complete)
Hi everyone,
I just wanted to remind you about tomorrow's meeting on the new telecommuting policies. The meeting will be at 10:00 a.m. in the second-level conference room. Please let me know if you can't attend.
See you then,

Course Slide Exercises

Exercise 1: The owner of a mutual fund stock wrote to the investment department of your bank, asking: "Just how does your bank want me to authorize this sale?"

Resolution Framework (5 Ws + 1 H): Your dynamic reply must outline: Who authorizes it (the account holder), What form is signed, Where to submit it, When it must arrive, Why validation is required, and How signatures are verified (e.g., via notary verification or secure portal upload).

Exercise 2: An out-of-town member writes: "I think I would like to attend my first meeting of the association, even though I am not acquainted with your city. Will you please tell me where the next meeting is being held?"

Resolution Framework: To make this complete, do not just name the venue. Provide the precise venue name, street address, map directions, nearby hotel options, parking arrangements, schedule timing, and contact coordinates.

Correctness

Definition: At the core of correctness is proper grammar, punctuation, and spelling. However, a business message is truly correct when it characteristics absolute factual precision, uses a proper level of language, and guarantees checking of all verified data metrics before transmission.

Core Rules of Correctness:
* Use the right level of language: Balance formal syntax against casual settings; never cross into slang or clinical terminology unless context requires it.
* Check accuracy of facts, figures, and words: A single misstated figure destroys transactional trust immediately.
* Maintain precision: Avoid ambiguity by defining names, numbers, and deadlines clearly.

2. Letter Writing (Full Block Format)

Structural Rules: In Full Block Format, every single textual element is strictly left-aligned to the margin. There is absolute zero paragraph indentation. Individual paragraphs are neatly divided using clean double-space line breaks to ensure high executive legibility.

[SENDER'S COMPANY NAME / LETTERHEAD]
123 Corporate Boulevard
Tech Suite 400
June 1, 2026
[INSIDE ADDRESS]
Mr. Jonathan Doe
Director of Operations
Acme Logistics Inc.
789 Supply Chain Way
Dear Mr. Doe:
[BODY PARAGRAPH 1 - NO INDENTATION]
This paragraph contains the primary objective of the letter, introducing the core topic immediately. Notice that every sentence begins flush against the strict left border with zero spaces inside.
[BODY PARAGRAPH 2 - NO INDENTATION]
This second paragraph expands on the supporting transactional facts, figures, or necessary chronological context. A uniform double-space separates it clearly from the preceding block text.
Sincerely,
[SENDER'S SIGNATURE]
Jane Smith
VP of Client Success

3. Persuasive Messages

Definition: Communications strategically structured to systematically influence an audience’s attitudes, core beliefs, or outward behavioral patterns to convince recipients to voluntarily adopt a specific viewpoint or take targeted execution paths.

Key Elements & Rhetorical Techniques

Technique / Element Core Concept & Academic Definition Practical Business Example
Ethos (Credibility) Establishes critical audience trust through certified expertise, verified reputation, or profound ethical alignment. "A board-certified medical specialist endorsing a health supplement to explicitly validate safety protocols."
Pathos (Emotion) Appeals deeply to visceral human emotions like empathy, shared joy, protective fear, or community pride to spark connection. "A localized nonprofit organization showing vulnerable imagery of families in need to encourage structural donations."
Logos (Logic) Deploys objective data, empirical facts, analytical research studies, and strict rational evidence frameworks to back claims. "An enterprise software engineering firm citing independent speed metrics showing a 40% gain in runtime efficiency."
Social Proof Leverages validation concepts by highlighting that broad public peers or market experts actively adopt this route. "Highlighting that over 50,000 active global tech companies rely daily on this custom integration suite."

Common Logical Fallacies (Must Avoid)

Slippery Slope

Falsely claiming a minor choice triggers a major, catastrophic sequence without logical steps.

Example: "If we give students calculators, soon they won’t learn math at all."

Bandwagon Appeal

Arguing an action is correct simply because a vast majority of people are currently doing it.

Example: "Everyone is switching to this brand, so it must be the best."

Hasty Generalization

Drawing sweeping conclusions using thin, unrepresentative, or localized evidence patches.

Example: "My neighbor got sick after eating at that restaurant, so it must be unsafe."

Appeal to Emotion

Manipulating feel-good sentiments or raw anxieties instead of introducing objective, logical reasoning elements.

Example: "If you don’t buy this product, you don’t care about your family’s safety."

Post Hoc (False Cause)

Incorrectly assuming that because event B occurred directly after event A, event A explicitly caused it.

Example: "I wore my lucky socks, and we won the game—so the socks caused the win."

Circular Reasoning

Using the basic premise as the actual conclusion to support itself without proving anything new.

Example: "This policy is the best because it’s better than all the others."

Red Herring

Introducing an entirely disconnected, sensationalist point to distract attention from the core issue.

Example: "Why worry about pollution when there are people who don’t have jobs?"

4. Product Review Writing

The Core Philosophy (Paul Barrs): Step one is picking a high-value, winning product. To establish real credibility, drop in budget-tier or entry-level product reviews from time to time so readers know you are honest and not simply trying to fleece them.

The Strategic Outline Structure

  • Know the Target Reader: Deeply analyze the demographic marketplace and tailor arguments to match group expectations.
  • Title Framework: Always format exactly as: Product Name: Product Review by [Your Qualifications to Review] for clear search engine layout identification.
  • Introduction: State a clear overview/opinion, outlining whether your assessment is ultimately positive or negative.
GCSE Writing Top Tips:
* Stay Focused: Constantly weigh your target product's value parameters against competitor items.
* Use Parenthesis: Drop extra commentary inside brackets (like this quick aside) to emphasize personal viewpoint authenticity.
* Think about your Tone: Confide directly and intimately in the reader to build immediate interpersonal trust.
* Rhetorical Questions: Ask directed questions and structure answers so your product feels like the only viable option.

Zenith X500 Noise-Cancelling Headphones: Product Review by an Audio Engineer
Published in Tech-Pulse Magazine • Target Audience: Audio Enthusiasts & Students

Introduction & Core Viewpoint:
Are you tired of losing your focus in noisy public study environments? The newly released Zenith X500 claims to offer studio-grade silencing properties, and after testing them extensively for a week, my definitive verdict is highly positive. This is an absolutely captivating piece of technology that sets a remarkable milestone for entry-level consumer electronics.

Comparative Analysis & Focus:
When weighed up against its closest competitor (the budget-tier SoundBlast 200), the Zenith X500 delivers significantly cleaner acoustics. While the SoundBlast feels uninspiring and produces a somewhat bland bass profile, the Zenith tracks sound frequencies with vivid, inventive audio dynamics. Why settle for low-grade sound reproduction when you can experience studio fidelity at nearly the same cost? Choosing the X500 seems like the only logical option for anyone serious about sound isolation.

The Final Recommendation:
To be completely honest, the headband adjustments can feel slightly awkward during prolonged wear. However, the sheer power of its active noise cancellation more than makes up for minor physical gripes. I highly recommend investing in this model before the current promotional tier ends.

5. Negotiation Skills

Definition: Back-and-forth communication designed to reach an agreement when you and the other side have some interests that are shared and others that are opposed. It serves as an interpersonal decision-making process required whenever we cannot achieve our objectives single-handedly.

Core Principles & Elements

  • Interdependence vs. Positions: Positions are what people say they want. Interests are why they want it. Focus entirely on underlying interests to uncover mutual gain options.
  • BATNA: Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement. This represents your ultimate source of leverage and walk-away power. Always calculate and strengthen your BATNA before coming to the negotiation table.

The Three Dynamics of Negotiation Conversations

Conversation Dynamic Strategic Matrix & Characteristics Behavioral Outcome
Win-Win (Integrative) Both sides cooperate to systematically expand the available value pool ("expand the pie") before executing any division. Focuses on shared interests and creative option generation. High voluntary commitment, stable long-term relationship health, and robust consensus.
Win-Lose (Distributive) A competitive, zero-sum environment. One party's gain directly translates to the opposing party's loss. Focuses strictly on claims over a fixed asset pool. Adversarial friction, low trust, and a compromised underlying relationship.
Lose-Lose An avoidant or highly rigid approach where both parties prioritize denying the other's objectives over discovering functional options. Stalemate, severe resource waste, and complete breakdown of consensus.

Phases of the Negotiation Process

  1. Preparation: Research the other party's interests, define your explicit targets, and establish a firm BATNA.
  2. Exploration & Discussion: Exchange structural data points, clarify core goals, and listen intently to discover hidden needs.
  3. Bargaining & Trade-Offs: Float open trial balloons, utilize the reciprocity principle, and offer smart concessions to generate mutual gain.
  4. Agreement & Implementation: Formulate specific, realistic, and completely documented commitments to finalize the deal.

6. Employment Interviews

Definition & Meaning: Derived from Latin and Middle French terms meaning to "see between" or "see each other". An interview is a formal meeting with a defined objective. The employer's core goal is to determine three critical criteria: Can you do the job? Will you do the job? How will you fit into the organization?

The Preparation Checklist

  • Due Diligence: Extensively research company operations, target market footprints, and organizational culture.
  • Arrival Protocol: Arrive exactly 10 to 15 minutes prior to the scheduled start time.
  • Starting Protocol: Greet the interviewer with a firm, confident handshake, maintain excellent eye contact, use upright posture, and project energy and enthusiasm.

Key Hiring Factors Considered by Interviewers

Hiring Factor Metric Percentage Weight Strategic Focus for Candidates
Work Experience 36% Quantify past technical results and align them directly with the target role.
First Impressions 24% Refine dress codes, professional posture, body language, and immediate energy.
Education 12% Highlight relevant academic focus areas and core functional projects.
Professional Qualifications 10% Showcase active industry certifications and specialized skill credentials.
References 9% Provide trusted endorsements that validate your professional integrity.

Standard Interview Questions & Graceful Exits

Standard Evaluation Questions
* Would you tell me about yourself?
* What is your greatest strength?
* What is your greatest weakness?
* Where do you see yourself in 5 years?
* Why should we hire you?
Questions You Should Ask
* What are your expectations of the employees in your company?
* Describe a typical day on the job in this position.
* Note: Avoid asking questions about salary and benefits early on.
The Graceful Exit & Follow-Up Protocol:
Conclude the meeting explicitly with professional sign-offs: "Thank you for your time. I appreciate it, and I will be looking forward to hearing from you." Evaluators expect you to complete your review performance check and send a professional thank-you note within 24 hours.
